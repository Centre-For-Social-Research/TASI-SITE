About the Festival

A First for India, A Milestone for the Global South

The Trust and Safety India Festival (TASI) 2025 emerged from a recognition that global conversations on digital safety have long been shaped by frameworks designed in and for the Global North. India, home to one of the world's largest and most diverse digital populations, needed its own platform to surface contextual priorities, advance local innovation, and contribute meaningfully to global governance.

Co-organized by the Centre for Social Research (CSR) and the Trust & Safety Forum, TASI 2025 was conceived as a multi-stakeholder, cross-sector, and collaborative space. It brought together government officials, international diplomats, technology companies, academics, civil society organizations, and digital rights advocates under a single unifying vision: building safer digital ecosystems through shared responsibility.

The festival was officially designated an Official Pre-Summit Event for India's AI Impact Summit (February 2026), underscoring how trust and safety have become foundational pillars of India's broader AI governance agenda at the global level.

Thematic Focus

Seven Key Tracks

Programming at TASI 2025 was organized around seven interconnected thematic tracks, each addressing a distinct but related dimension of digital safety in modern societies.

01
🤖
AI Governance & Safety

Building ethical, accountable, and inclusive AI frameworks rooted in transparency and global cooperation. Discussions included India's AI Safety Institute, bias mitigation tools, watermarking, and deepfake detection.

02
🧒
Child Protection

Designing safer digital environments for children grounded in privacy-first defaults, age-appropriate design, and protection from online sexual abuse material and grooming.

03
🌐
Youth Wellbeing

Supporting young people's healthy engagement with digital spaces — balancing participation, autonomy, and safety as they navigate online environments shaped by algorithmic logic.

04
⚖️
Gendered & Sexualized Harms

Addressing technology-facilitated gender-based violence (TFGBV), image-based abuse, non-consensual intimate image sharing, and the emerging forms of exploitation enabled by AI tools.

05
💙
Trust & Safety Workforce

Recognizing the emotional labor of content moderation teams, prioritizing mental health infrastructure, and advancing "Care by Design" as a benchmark for platform operations.

06
🛡️
Safety by Design

Embedding safety, transparency, and accountability into technologies and AI systems from the outset, ensuring user protection is integral throughout the design and deployment process.

07
🤝
Platform Responsibility & Cross-Sector Collaboration

Strengthening cooperation among governments, civil society, and technology platforms to address online harms systematically — with a focus on interoperability, transparency reporting, and shared accountability frameworks.

Inaugural Keynote

India's Foreign Minister Opens the Festival

Dr. S. Jaishankar, Minister of External Affairs, delivered the inaugural keynote address at TASI 2025, marking one of the highest-level endorsements of trust and safety as a national policy priority. His participation positioned the festival at the intersection of diplomacy, technology governance, and digital rights.

In his remarks, Dr. Jaishankar articulated how artificial intelligence will transform economies, health systems, education, and everyday life in ways that are all-pervasive and not merely sectoral. He emphasized that India bears a special responsibility as a leader for the Global South, where digital public infrastructure has already demonstrated what accountable, scalable technology can achieve.

"Technology is a force for good, but only if humanity guides it."

Dr. S. Jaishankar, quoting PM Narendra Modi, TASI 2025 Keynote

He called for adequate guardrails to protect "digital nagriks" (digital citizens), stressing that concerns about bias, ethics, privacy, and vulnerability are well-founded and must shape India's approach to AI governance at home and in global forums.

Research & Data Spotlights

The Numbers Behind the Urgency

Evidence presented at TASI 2025 made a compelling case for why India urgently needs contextual, locally-grounded digital safety frameworks.

90%+
TFGBV Data Unreported
Across South Asia, over 90% of technology-facilitated gender-based violence data remains unreported or unstructured, pointing to massive gaps in documentation and redressal infrastructure.
30%
Children Talking with Strangers Online
Research from the British Asia Trust found that while 99% of children have device access, 30% admit to engaging with strangers online, and 22% have met them in person, with few knowing how to report abuse.
25%
LGBTQ+ TFGBV Survivors at Risk
Among LGBTQ+ respondents who experienced TFGBV in an 18-country study, 25% were more likely to contemplate self-harm, revealing acute vulnerabilities that current platforms and laws fail to address.
10 Days+
Platform Response Times for Child Safety
Corporate redressal mechanisms were found to take over ten days to act on serious child-safety complaints, a timeline that panelists described as unacceptable given the severity of harm.
1,500+
Content Takedowns via Meri Trust Line
India's dedicated helpline for image-based abuse, Meri Trust Line (6363-176363), has facilitated over 2,000 calls and 1,500 successful content takedowns since its 2023 launch.
$20B+
Meta's Investment in Online Safety
Technology partners at TASI demonstrated institutional commitment: Meta alone has invested over $20 billion in online safety technology and launched more than 50 dedicated safety tools and features.
Hands-On Learning

Workshops & Immersive Sessions

Beyond panels, TASI 2025 featured interactive workshops where participants engaged directly with tools, scenarios, and co-design exercises to produce practical knowledge and actionable frameworks.

Workshop 01 · Oct 7
Designing Safer Pathways: GroSafe's AI Chatbot for Child Protection
Dylan Schouten, GroSafe at TU Dublin (EU-funded)

Introduced GroSafe, an AI-powered child protection tool that operates as a signposting agent, not a conversational bot. It detects signals of grooming or abuse and directs young users to appropriate helplines, operating from a closed server to prevent misuse. Attendees tested the chatbot and provided live design feedback, debating the balance between speed and sensitivity.

Ethical AI must support early intervention without replacing human care networks. People seek help when they feel safe, not when they feel watched.
Workshop 02 · Oct 7
Strategic Foresight: Future-Proofing Trust
Farshida Zafar, Erasmus Centre for Entrepreneurship

Taught a six-step foresight methodology including PESTEL analysis, scenario crafting, and reverse-engineering of responses. Participants developed scenarios for 2035 ranging from a "Global Digital Accord" to an "AI Wild West," exploring how centralization, decentralization, and AI autonomy would reshape trust and safety ecosystems.

Outliers, not trends, most often destabilize trust. Generative AI is an outlier that requires proactive planning, not just reaction.
Workshop 03 · Oct 7
Care, Not Control: An Intergenerational Dialogue on Teens' Online Safety
Aparajita Bharti, Kevin Lee (Yuvaa), Sagar Deoskar (Snap)

An open, non-judgmental forum under "Vegas Rule" confidentiality that placed teens' voices at the center. Teens described care as a supportive "safety net" and control as stifling pressure. Parents raised concerns about digital literacy gaps. The session surfaced that digital well-being is relational and requires shared boundary-setting, not surveillance.

Care is a net, not a cage. Digital safety must be co-created between youth, caregivers, platforms, and policymakers.
Workshop 04 · Oct 8
Reimagining Safety: Youth, Tech, and Active Citizenship
Sonali Vyas, Safetipin

Used Safetipin's interactive crowd-sourced mapping tools to demonstrate how real-time, citizen-generated data can shape safer cities. Participants stepped into the perspectives of different users (young commuter, professional, elderly teacher) to map how safety varies by gender, age, and ability, and discussed the digital risks embedded in navigation technologies.

Youth-led safety innovations prioritize community agency over institutional control. Active citizenship emerges when young people have platforms to document and advocate.
Workshop 05 · Oct 8
Sandboxing a Regulatory Sandbox for Child Online Safety
Dr. Ioanna Noula, Children's Online Redress (COR) Sandbox

Introduced the COR Sandbox as an experimental regulatory space where youth, academics, technologists, and lawyers co-test policy ideas and complaint systems before they solidify into law. Featured an India–Greece partnership model that blends cultural context with strong privacy norms, and emphasized that grievance mechanisms must be simple, child-friendly, and not bureaucratic.

Participation is protection. Sandboxes let law and policy evolve faster than harm by embedding child feedback into design and governance.
Roundtable 06 · Oct 8
#CreateWithCare: India's Content Ecosystem & Responsible Practices
Organized by Alliance for Cyber Trust & Safety (ACTS)

A closed-door roundtable for 30+ creators, platform policy leaders, and educators. Addressed wellness content created without professional training, India's weak disclosure culture for sponsored posts, and the algorithmic logic of virality that rewards speed over safety. An online gaming sector representative shared how proactive self-governance prevented heavier government intervention.

Care isn't censorship; it's competence with conscience. Proactive creator accountability reduces the need for reactive external regulation.

Building on TASI 2025

Key Recommendations for India's Digital Future

TASI 2025 generated a comprehensive set of actionable recommendations, grounded in two days of substantive multi-stakeholder dialogue, to advance digital safety across India and the Global South.

REC 01
Deepen Civil Society-Led Collaboration as a Catalyst for Innovation

TASI proved that civil society-convened platforms can unlock collaboration between government, industry, academia, and communities. Companies and regulators must deepen investment in these collaborative spaces as ongoing partners, not occasional participants, to co-create equitable digital governance models.

REC 02
Move from Compliance to Co-Design

Participants widely affirmed readiness to move beyond compliance-driven engagement toward actively co-designing solutions. The next phase demands structured forums, working groups, and co-creation mechanisms that channel this readiness into tangible, implementable outcomes.

REC 03
Expand Cross-Industry Participation Beyond Traditional Platforms

Digital safety challenges transcend platform boundaries — fraud spans fintech and social media, youth safety touches gaming and streaming, misinformation flows across news and commerce. Gaming, telecom, fintech, edtech, and e-commerce sectors must join these conversations and bring their operational insights to bear on shared challenges.

REC 04
Prioritize India-First Innovation for Global Influence

India's unique cultural, linguistic, and social context demands homegrown frameworks — not imported playbooks. Companies investing in India-centric research, localized product development, and culturally grounded safety mechanisms will serve Indian users better while pioneering models that resonate across emerging markets globally.

REC 05
Develop Coordinated AI Governance Frameworks

As generative AI and algorithmic systems become embedded across sectors, the gap between technical capability and governance readiness grows acute. Dedicated AI safety working groups should jointly develop practical governance frameworks, share red-teaming insights, and pilot transparency mechanisms that work at Indian scale.

REC 06
Establish Year-Round Sustained Engagement Structures

Progress requires consistent collaboration, not just an annual convening. Working groups, innovation labs, and recurring check-ins will enable stakeholders to prototype approaches, track shared progress, and iterate on emerging challenges — ensuring safety is a continuous responsibility rather than a crisis response.

REC 07
Embed Survivor-Centered Design in Platform Architecture

Across TFGBV, child protection, and moderation, participants consistently called for reporting and redressal systems designed for and with survivors — not just for regulatory compliance. Trauma-informed, stigma-aware, and co-created redressal processes must replace bureaucratic, retraumatizing mechanisms.

REC 08
Institutionalize Care by Design for Trust & Safety Workers

Research presented at TASI found that 11% of moderation workers develop measurable anxiety disorders. Surface-level wellness programs are insufficient. "Care by Design" must be embedded in operational DNA through peer mentoring, rotational duties, non-stigmatizing support spaces, and early trauma intervention benchmarks.

What Comes Next

Looking Ahead to TASI 2026

TASI 2025 concluded not with a final word, but with an invitation. The conversations, partnerships, and commitments forged in New Delhi are the foundation for an even more ambitious second edition, scheduled for October 2026.

With India's AI Impact Summit having taken place in February 2026, TASI 2026 will carry those global conversations forward in a more focused and practice-oriented way — building on international momentum while remaining firmly rooted in India's social, cultural, and digital realities.

🌍
Stronger Global South Leadership
Consolidating TASI's identity as a space where emerging economies define their own priorities. Building South-South learning networks and a shared vocabulary for digital safety across diverse contexts.
📊
Trust & Safety Index
A proposed benchmark capturing year-on-year progress across AI governance, TFGBV mitigation, crisis management, and institutional capacity — creating public accountability for the entire ecosystem.
🏙️
Regional & State-Level Dialogues
Expanding beyond Delhi to reach youth, communities, schools, and universities across India's regions, ensuring digital safety conversations are responsive to diverse geographies and lived experiences.
🔬
Implementation Clinics & Innovation Showcases
New formats for 2026 will feature solution-focused roundtables, hands-on clinics, and safety-tech showcases that move from dialogue to concrete action and knowledge exchange.
🏭
Broader Industry Participation
Welcoming telecommunications, gaming, fintech, edtech, e-commerce, and media sectors to deepen cross-industry understanding of digital safety challenges that transcend traditional platform boundaries.
